A B S T R A C T

In the present work, a computer implementation of the localizing gradient damage method (LGDM) is presented in a commercial finite element (FE) package, COMSOL. The LGDM is adopted because it can accurately model damage/fracture initiation and propagation. The accuracy of LGDM is due to its thermodynamically consistent formulation using the micromorphic framework. Among available FE implementation software, the COMSOL is preferred for current implementation due to its several advantages. Firstly, the COMSOL user interface (UI) is highly intuitive and informative because, unlike other software, it displays the mathematical basis for every option in the UI. Using this, someone with a knowledge of the mathematical theory of FEM can master COMSOL quickly without any prior experience. Secondly, many customization options available in COMSOL are helpful for advanced users who intend to implement new models. Thirdly, COMSOL's equation-based modelling feature is handy while implementing a new nonlinear coupled field model, like LGDM. It is shown in this work that COMSOL only needs the strong form of PDE to solve and performs weak formulation/linearization of the PDE in the background. This drastically reduces the implementation time for highly nonlinear models because users only need to provide the PDE's strong form to COMSOL. Apart from these advantages, the COMSOL implementation is computationally efficient and is capable of solving complex/large scale problems with minimal computational resources. A detailed discussion on the COMSOL implementation for a nonlinear model is presented and sample simulation files are provided as supplementary material that would be helpful to users/researchers working on similar coupled field models.

1. Introduction

The numerical modelling and simulation of fracture has recently become a widely researched area in applied mechanics. This is evident from the multitude of numerical methods developed for fracture modelling in the past few decades. These methods can be broadly categorized into two groups based on their origins, i.e., fracture mechanics-based methods and damage mechanics-based methods. Some of the fracture mechanics-based methods include the extended finite element method [1-3], extended isogeometric analysis [4], extended element free Galerkin method [5] among others. While the non-local damage method [6], phase field method [7,8] and gradient damage method [9,10] belong to the category of damage mechanics-based methods.

The ability of these aforementioned methods to accurately model and simulate fracture phenomena comes at the cost of their increased complexity. In other words, accurate fracture modelling is possible due to complex algorithms used by these methods, such as coupled-field procedures, nonlinear solution techniques and complex constitutive modelling. Due to these complexities, the fracture simulations become cumbersome, especially using in-house developed codes. Moreover, in-house developed codes are primarily suitable for the specific problems they are designed to address and often are computationally inefficient. Besides, the in-house developed codes become irrelevant and unusable with time due to limited maintainability and applicability.
In order to increase the applicability and efficiency of the fracture modelling methods, numerous finite element (FE) implementations in commercial software packages, like ABAQUS, ANSYS and COMSOL, have recently gained traction. These FE implementations in commercial packages offer several benefits, such as availability of highly optimized standard libraries (such as element libraries, mesher routines, solver routines, assembly routines and many more), availability of visualization toolboxes and availability of a large online community for troubleshooting. Additionally, these implementations help in the faster development and adoption of any novel simulation method. For example, the initial FE implementations of methods like extended FEM [11] and phase field method [12,13] ,which also shared the source codes,led to the rapid development of these methods and increased adoption by a larger group of researchers. Among available commercial software, ABAQUS and COMSOL are popular for FE implementations relevant to fracture modelling. Various works on implementation in ABAQUS and COMSOL are discussed in the following.
The FE implementations in ABAQUS are carried out using user-defined codes [14]. In ABAQUS, the user-defined codes are mainly written as user subroutines in the FORTRAN programming language. The ABAQUS implementations for fracture modelling started with Giner et al. [11], in which the extended FEM was implemented in the ABAQUS. Later, this work on XFEM was extended to the 3D modelling in ABAQUS by Shi et al. [15]. Further, the isogeometric analysis (IGA) was implemented in ABAQUS by Elguedj et al. [16]. In another work, Park and Paulino [17] implemented the cohesive zone model (CZM) in ABAQUS for modelling of crack propagation. Recently ABAQUS implementations have been frequently used in the phase field method (PFM) of fracture [18], Molnar and Gravouil (2017); Fang et al. [19]. Apart from these, the ABAQUS implementation has been used for peridynamics [20], cell-based smoothed FEM [21] and virtual element method [22]. Recently, there have been two ABAQUS implementations for the gradient damage method by Seupel et al. [23] and Sarkar et al. [24,25]. In COMSOL, the FE implementations for fracture are mostly recent that discuss the implementation of PFM for quasi-static, dynamic - multiphysics fracture [13,26] and implementation of XFEM for solid and multiphase models [27,28] .
In the present work, a recently developed fracture modelling method called the localizing gradient damage method (LGDM) is adopted for FE implementation. The adoption of LGDM is based on its versatility and advantages over other fracture modelling methods [29]; Sarkar et al., [24,30,31]. To utilize these advantages, a simplified implementation of LGDM is presented in the commercial FE software COMSOL. In this work, the COMSOL implementation is simplified and becomes easy to understand due to the following reasons,
  • Unlike other commercial software like ABAQUS and ANSYS, the GUI (graphical user interface) based implementation in COMSOL eliminates the mandatory requirement of coding skills for the user. In other words, writing codes (user subroutines) is not necessary for a COMSOL implementation.
  • The strong forms of governing equations are directly used in COMSOL implementation. This eliminates the requirement of weak formulation and linearization by the user for a nonlinear problem.
  • The solution dependent variables (like damage, equivalent strain, etc.) can be defined and tracked (for debugging) in a convenient manner using the GUI. In other words, the user effort is saved from variable handling, which is an unproductive implementation overhead.
Besides the simplifications offered by COMSOL, a considerable increase in the computational efficiency is also observed in the simulations. Due to the simplifications and computational efficiency, 3D fracture simulations are carried out that is otherwise infeasible using 2D representations. Moreover, the multi-physics capabilities of COMSOL can be used to extend the present framework to model various multi-physics fracture phenomena.
This paper is organized into five sections. Section 1 (introduction) provides a brief review of existing FE implementations in fracture modelling and highlights the motivation and benefits of the present work. In Section 2, a brief review of the localizing gradient damage method highlights the necessary formulation required in the implementation. Subsequently, in Section 3, a brief overview of the COMSOL implementation methodology for LGDM is presented, and interested readers are pointed to the Appendix section for a more detailed discussion. Section 4 demonstrates the capability of the COMSOL implementation through various 1D, 2D and 3D numerical examples. Finally, Section 5 concludes the paper by highlighting the major conclusions.

2. Localizing gradient damage method

In this section, a review of the localizing gradient damage method (LGDM) is presented. This is a brief review that focuses on the aspects that are important for COMSOL implementation. Unlike other gradient damage methods, the LGDM is a thermodynamically consistent fracture modelling method that can accurately capture the effect of fracture processes taking place at the underlying micro-continuum [29]. The effect of fracture processes occurring at the underlying micro-continuum are included in the model through a morphic variable. The introduction of this morphic variable in addition to the existing kinematic field variables is called the micro-morphic framework [32]. In other words, the micromorphic framework accounts for these fluctuating responses at the microlevel that are otherwise neglected in the macroscopic continuum theory.
In the micromorphic framework,the free energy density is assumed such that the energy due to higher-order stresses (σ¯and ξ¯) associated with the morphic variable (ε¯eq) and its gradient (∇ε¯eq) are also considered. The free energy density (ψ) is expressed as,
(1)Ψ=12(1−D)ε:4C:ε+12h(εeq−ε¯eq)2+12ghc(∇ε¯eq∇ε¯eq)
The first term in Eq. (1) represents the standard elastic strain energy using 4C as the fourth-order elasticity tensor and a scalar damage variable (D) for material damage. The second and third terms in Eq. (1) are non-standard and physically signify the micro-macro and micro-micro interactions respectively. The macro and micro-equivalent strains in the second term (εeq−ε¯eq) account for micro-macro interactions whose magnitude is quantified by a parameter called the coupling modulus denoted by h . The contribution of micro-micro interactions occurring at the micro-continuum are included through the gradient of the micro-equivalent strains (∇ε¯eq) . In addition to coupling modulus (h) ,a couple of additional parameters are defined in the third term,viz. the gradient parameter (c) and the interaction parameter (g) . A detailed discussion of these parameters is undertaken in the previous works of the authors [30,31,33,34] and is avoided here for brevity.
The constitutive relations are obtained by following the Coleman-Noll procedure on the free energy density function (ψ) as,
(2)σ=(1−D)C:ε+h(εeq−ε¯eq)∂εeq∂ε
(3)σ¯=h(ε¯eq−εeq)
(4)ξ¯=ghc(∇ε¯eq)

3. COMSOL implementation

The implementation of LGDM in the COMSOL follows the procedure of a typical nonlinear finite element analysis. This procedure involves three tasks, namely, pre-processing, processing and post-processing. These tasks are highlighted using a snapshot of the Model Builder in Fig. 2. The Model Builder is divided into multiple sections to highlight various individual activities carried out under each task. From the sections of Model Builder following important points are noted,
  • In the current COMSOL implementation, most of the activities required to be performed by the user are under pre-processing task. This implies minimal user involvement in processing and post-processing tasks, thus, saves time and effort.
  • The wide applicability of the COMSOL implementation is highlighted by relatively fewer modifications necessary when different problems are solved. For example, the Sub-Nodes Parameters, Geometry, Loads/Boundary Conditions (Under Solid Mechanics) and Meshing are the ones that are only modified to simulate different problems, while, rest of the Nodes/Sub-Nodes remain unchanged.
  • The weak formulation and linearization of the governing equations are carried out in the background by the COMSOL. However, to ensure the correctness and consistency of the derived weak form and linearization, the variable definitions in the Sub-Node Variables must be correct.
A detailed discussion on various features of COMSOL used in the present implementation is provided in the Appendix. The discussion in the Appendix uses COMSOL graphical user interface (GUI) for clarity and easy understanding. In this section, the versatility of the COMSOL implementation for LGDM is demonstrated through several 1D, 2D and 3D numerical problems. These numerical problems are adopted such that their reference or experimental results are available for verification. Moreover, the computational efficiency achieved by the COMSOL implementation is shown through a comparison with MATLAB implementation of LGDM. Starting with a 1D problem in the first sub-section, three numerical problems are considered for each 2D and 3D case in the subsequent sub-sections.

4.3. 2D shear band problem (Compression Load)

In this sub-section, the shear band problem is used to demonstrate the capability of the 2D COMSOL implementation of LGDM to simulate failure under compressive load. The geometry, loads, boundary conditions and parameters are shown in Fig. 12. The material and numerical parameters used for this problem are adopted from a similar problem solved in Sarkar et al. [33]. A defect region is defined at the lower-left corner of the specimen with a 50% reduction in strength to trigger damage,as shown in Fig. 12. A uniform mesh and displacement-controlled load in equal load-steps of 0.01mm are used for the simulation. All the dimensions of the problem are in mm .
The obtained load-displacement response is shown in Fig. 13. It is observed that mesh convergence is achieved at the mesh density of 14,400 elements and the converged load-displacement curve matches well with the reference problem [33]. Besides, the obtained damage and micro-equivalent strain plots are shown in Fig. 14.
Formation of the shear band is observed in the damage plots and intense localization (observed through micro-equivalent strain) within the band implies failure along the shear band. The shear band is found to initiate from the defect region and subsequently leads to complete failure consistent with results in published literature [29,35] . The accuracy of the load-displacement response and consistency of the shear band formation highlights the effectiveness of COMSOL implementation of LGDM in 2D under compression load.

It is observed that the damage initially starts along the inclined notch tip and goes on to become perpendicular to the frontal surface, thus, resulting in a twisted crack/damage path. Apart from these, the load-displacement response shown in Fig. 26 demonstrates the mesh convergence of the results.

5. Conclusion

A COMSOL implementation of the localizing gradient damage method (LGDM) is presented in the present work for simulating fracture. Various implementation aspects of the LGDM are discussed in detail through the snapshots of the COMSOL user interface. The detailed discussion includes all the essential aspects of the COMSOL user interface relevant to the implementation of LGDM. Several 1D, 2D and 3D numerical problems are solved to demonstrate the accuracy and superior capability of the current implementation. The results obtained from the COMSOL implementation are compared with reference/experimental results to ascertain their validity. Few important conclusions drawn from the present work are as follows,
  • The COMSOL implementation based on the graphical user interface (GUI) is straightforward and easy to understand. In other words, COMSOL's GUI based implementation is simpler than code-based implementations in other software.
  • The COMSOL implementation uses highly optimised algorithms and procedures that lead to higher computational efficiency. The computational efficiency is achieved by increasing the CPU usage, leading to reduced computation time and reduced physical memory (RAM) usage. This is evident from the comparison with both MATLAB and ABAQUS based implementations. It is also found that, despite using slightly higher RAM than ABAQUS, COMSOL can achieve higher overall performance among all three software.
  • The simplicity of the COMSOL implementation led to seamless implementation in 1D, 2D and 3D. More specifically, after completing the 1D implementation in COMSOL, the implementation in 2D and 3D are straightforward.
  • The results obtained from the COMSOL implementation of LGDM are consistent with reference/experimental results. It is found that the COMSOL implementation of LGDM accurately and efficiently simulates complex crack patterns in 3D.
Lastly, it is highlighted that the comparisons in this paper are presented to encourage users/researchers to adopt COMSOL due to its scalability, efficiency and simplicity. In other words, COMSOL can be used to scale simulations that have been already prototyped in a simple general-purpose framework like MATLAB. An even better approach would be to use COMSOL directly for prototyping, as its user interface is simple and intuitive. Using COMSOL directly would save a lot of time that goes into debugging in other code-based implementation frameworks. It is acknowledged that there may be cases where ABAQUS may have higher overall performance than COMSOL. However, it is important to consider the simplicity of implementation and debugging in computational modelling while selecting an implementation framework. With a simple and intuitive user interface, COMSOL can be considered a better alternative than ABAQUS.

Appendix A

COMSOL graphical user interface (GUI)

COMSOL Multiphysics (COMSOL) is a finite element analysis software that can perform uncoupled and coupled analysis of various physical phenomena such as structural, thermal, fluids and many more [40]. The multi-physics capabilities of the COMSOL make it a preferred analysis software in many industrial and academic research fields. In the COMSOL, all the functions of a typical FE analysis can be performed using both graphical user interface (GUI) or the command-line interface (CLI). However, the CLI is only available when COMSOL is integrated with MATLAB [41] during the installation process. Nonetheless, owing to a versatile GUI, numerically complex user-defined models can be quickly built in the COMSOL without writing a single line of code. This drastically reduces the implementation and debugging time. Moreover, COMSOL also supports in-simulation visualization, thus, further decreasing the debugging time.
The graphical user interface (GUI) of the COMSOL consists of five major parts as shown in different colour shades in Fig. A.1. These parts are described as,

LGDM implementation

Although the COMSOL package has numerous physics modules, the present work particularly focuses on the Structural Mechanics and Mathematics modules. The Structural Mechanics module has all the functionality to perform static or quasi-static analysis, while the Mathematics module enables modelling through PDEs, ODEs and several other options. A snapshot showing an expanded view of the Structural Mechanics and Mathematics module is depicted in Fig. A.2. In this work, the standard equilibrium equation (7) is defined through the Structural Mechanics module and the microforce balance equation (8) is defined through the Mathematics module. Solid Mechanics is chosen from the options available in the Structural Mechanics module for the standard equilibrium equation (Fig. A.2). In the Mathematics module, the Helmholtz Equation is adopted for the microforce balance equation under the Classical PDEs (Fig. A.2). The use of Helmholtz Equation for modelling of microforce balance equation is inspired by a similar COMSOL implementation of phase field method in Zhou et al. [13].

The overall structure

The overall structure of FE implementation in COMSOL can be understood from the Model Builder interface shown in Fig. A.3a. It can be observed that the COMSOL models are saved with a * . mph file extension shown at the root (topmost) level. Below the root level, several major Nodes (drop-down arrows) are present: Global Definitions, Component, Study and Results. Under these Nodes, various SubNodes include the definitions for the model. Several important Nodes and Sub-Nodes are discussed in the following,
  • Parameters Sub-Node (under Global Definitions Node): The Parameters Sub-Node is used to define all the required material or numerical parameters used globally (i.e., applicable for the entire domain) and would remain constant during the entire time history of the simulation. The parameters used in the LGDM formulations are shown in the properties of Parameters in Fig. A.3b. It is noted that the units of the parameters are written within the square brackets and should be consistent throughout the model.
  • Definitions Sub-Node (under Component Node): Within the Definitions Sub-Node, Variables and State Variables options are used in the current implementation. All the solution dependent variables (εeq,D,g) are defined in the Variables,whereas in State Variables,the history strain parameter (κ¯) is defined. The definitions are shown in Fig. A.4b, c. It is noted that the expressions used in evaluating the solution dependent variables are written directly under the expression column.
Some of the important points relevant to the Definitions Sub-Node are highlighted in the following,
o The Variables are defined in the order they would be evaluated during the simulation (Fig. A.4b). For example, a variable named 10c_e_eqv (for εeq ) can only be defined when I1 and J2 are evaluated as per Eq. (16); thus, I1 and J2 will come above the loc_e_eqv.
o For the incremental-iterative solution scheme, the linearization performed by COMSOL uses different variables defined in the Variables (Fig. A.4b). Therefore, it is important to check these variables definitions with respect to linearization. For example, any variable definition leading to undefined values during a partial differentiation for linearization must be appropriately checked and modified for correctness.
o The definition of history parameter (κ¯) using the State Variables is found to be more efficient and straightforward (Fig. A.4c), as compared to the ODE and DAE Interface (Mathematics module) used in Zhou et al. [13] for the phase field method. The use of ODE and DAE Interface leads to unwanted computational overhead due to the formulation and solving of another differential equation. In contrast, the State Variables definition is computationally efficient as it only requires very little additional memory and does not need any formulation or solving of a differential equation.
o The use of conditional expressions (if-else statements) can be noted in the definitions of Variables and State Variables.
  • Geometry, Solid Mechanics, Microforce balance, Mesh Sub-Nodes (under Component Node): Several Sub-Nodes under Component are depicted in Fig. A.5. The snapshots show various options used under each Sub-Node. The Geometry Sub-Node is used to either import or create a geometry. Various steps taken during a CAD modelling carried out in COMSOL are listed under Geometry (Fig. A.5b). The Sub-Nodes Solid Mechanics and Microforce balance are discussed later in detail. The Mesh Sub-Node is used for generating the FE mesh. It has numerous controls that can generate a mesh of desired resolution and contain desired elements (Fig. A.5e). Apart from the options shown in the snapshots, several other options can also be used for creating models whose details can be found in COMSOL online documentation [40].
  • Study and Results Nodes: These are the next major Nodes used to configure solver preferences and visualize results. Different available options in these Nodes are shown in Fig. A.6. Several important points related to the Study and Results are as follows,
Fig. A.6. Screenshots showing Study and Results Nodes used in COMSOL implementation.
o The solver preferences like convergence tolerances, error estimates, load-step size, maximum iteration count etc., are defined in the
Study. The load-step size is defined using a load-step parameter that varies from 0 to 1 within Step 1: Stationary Sub-Node.
o In the Study, two levels below the Solver Configurations, a Sub-Node called Fully Coupled can be used to change convergence tolerance and the method of termination in the event of divergence.

The Solid Mechanics Sub-Node (under Component Node)

The Solid Mechanics Sub-Node is used to define material parameters, discretization order, loads and boundary conditions for a structural analysis (solving the standard equilibrium equation (7)) of a particular problem. Various options available in the Solid Mechanics Sub-Node are shown in Fig. A.7a. These options include the Linear Elastic Material, Initial Values, Prescribed Displacement, Boundary Loads and Symmetry. In the LGDM,the scalar damage variable (D) is introduced through the constitutive equation (2). This constitutive equation is incorporated in the COMSOL implementation through the Linear Elastic Material option (Fig. A.7b). It can be seen that the scalar damage variable is introduced in the definition of Young's Modulus. It is highlighted that the coupling term (second term) in the constitutive equation (2) is not included in the definition due to its insignificant contribution [24,34].
The other options such as Initial Values, Prescribed Displacement, Boundary Loads and Symmetry are used to apply initial and boundary conditions as per the requirement of the problem. Apart from these, the discretization order for the Solid Mechanics Node is selected through its Settings shown in Fig. A.7c. It is noted that Quadratic Serendipity elements are adopted for the discretization of the displacement in the Solid Mechanics Node.
